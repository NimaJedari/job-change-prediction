Section 2

Data quality assessment & cleaning

The raw dataset contained naturally occurring missing values across multiple columns — a realistic reflection of how HR and recruiting data arrives in practice. Each column required a deliberate, context-aware cleaning decision rather than a blanket strategy.

Missing values — raw dataset

Cleaning decisions

gender — 4,508 missing (23.5%)
Filled with "Unknown" rather than imputing a value. Non-disclosure is a meaningful category in itself and should not be silently replaced with the majority class.
company_type — 6,140 missing (32.1%)
Filled with "Unknown". At 32% missing, imputing with mode would heavily distort the distribution. Missingness here signals candidates with no current employer — a strong behavioral signal.
company_size — 5,938 missing (31.0%)
Ordinal encoded (<10→1 through 10000+→8), with 0 assigned to missing rows. Zero meaningfully represents "no company" rather than a random imputed size.
major_discipline — 2,813 missing (14.7%)
Filled with "No Major" — a structurally meaningful category for candidates without a university degree (High School / Primary School education levels).
experience — string with ">20" and "<1"
Converted to numeric: ">20"→21, "<1"→0. 65 remaining NaN values imputed with median (5.0 years). A classic real-world data entry issue requiring string parsing before numeric conversion.
last_new_job — string with "never" and ">4"
Converted to numeric: "never"→0, ">4"→5. 423 remaining NaN values imputed with median (1.0). Same string-to-numeric conversion pattern as experience.
education_level — 460 missing (2.4%)
Small enough for mode imputation (Graduate). Then ordinally encoded: Primary School=1, High School=2, Graduate=3, Masters=4, PhD=5 — preserving the natural ordering that one-hot encoding would lose.
training_hours — log transformation
Raw skewness of 1.819 reduced to −0.346 after log1p transformation. Heavy right tail compressed, improving feature separability for the model.
enrollee_id & city — dropped
enrollee_id is a pure identifier with no predictive value. city has 123 unique categories — replaced entirely by city_development_index which captures the same geographic signal continuously.
enrolled_university — 386 missing (2.0%)
Small enough for safe mode imputation (no_enrollment). Pattern is consistent with candidates who completed their education and are now working.

Section 3

Feature engineering

Three engineered features were created to capture non-linear relationships and interaction effects that raw features alone cannot express. Each was motivated by domain reasoning about candidate job-seeking behavior.

cdi_bin — city development index bins
city_development_index cut into 4 ordinal bands: low (<0.624), mid (0.624–0.789), high (0.789–0.920), very_high (>0.920). The relationship between CDI and job switching is non-linear — binning captures threshold effects that a continuous variable misses.
is_unemployed — binary flag
Set to 1 where company_type = "Unknown" AND company_size = 0 simultaneously. Identifies candidates with no current employer — 5,360 flagged (28.0%). Became the single most important feature at 25.7% importance.
exp_x_company_size — interaction term
experience × company_size. A senior engineer at a startup (high exp, low size) has very different switching behavior from a junior at a large corporation (low exp, high size). The interaction captures this directly.
education_level_ord — ordinal encoding
Replaced one-hot encoding of education_level with a 1–5 ordinal scale (Primary→PhD). Preserves the natural hierarchy and reduces feature dimensionality from 5 binary columns to 1 numeric column.
Impact: Feature engineering increased Switch Recall from 74% to 77.1% — catching 35 additional candidates per 3,832 evaluated. At scale across a 50,000-candidate pipeline, this represents ~460 additional correctly identified job-seekers.
Section 4

Class imbalance & SMOTE

75.1% of candidates in the dataset did not switch jobs, creating a meaningful class imbalance that would cause a naive model to systematically underpredict job switchers.

Original class distribution

Stay — 75.1% (14,381)
Switch — 24.9% (4,777)

SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) was applied exclusively to the training set after the train/test split, generating synthetic Switch examples by interpolating between existing minority class observations. The test set remained at its natural distribution for honest evaluation.

After SMOTE — training set

Stay — 50% (11,504)
Switch — 50% (11,504)

Training set grew from 15,326 → 23,008 rows. Test set: 3,832 rows, original distribution preserved.

Critical design decision: SMOTE was applied only after splitting. Applying it before would contaminate the test set with synthetic data derived from training observations, producing artificially inflated metrics that do not reflect real-world performance.
Section 5

Model training & hyperparameter tuning

An XGBoost classifier was trained on the SMOTE-balanced training set. GridSearchCV with 5-fold cross-validation tested 80 hyperparameter combinations, scoring each by ROC-AUC.

Parameters searched (16 combinations)
n_estimators: [100, 200] · max_depth: [3, 4] · learning_rate: [0.05, 0.1] · subsample: [0.8, 1.0]
Best parameters found
n_estimators: 200 · max_depth: 4 · learning_rate: 0.1 · subsample: 0.8 · CV ROC-AUC: 0.9082
subsample: 0.8 was selected over 1.0 — the model performs better using 80% of training rows per tree. This introduces regularising randomness that prevents memorising SMOTE-generated synthetic patterns, keeping generalisation strong on real test data.

Threshold tuning

The default classification threshold of 0.50 was lowered to 0.39 by sweeping all thresholds from 0.10 to 0.70 and selecting the value that maximised F1 for the Switch class. This trades a small number of additional false positives for meaningfully higher Switch recall.

Section 8

Key findings & recruiting implications

Unemployment is the strongest predictor of job switching — candidates with no current employer accounted for 25.7% of total model importance. In a recruiting context this means unemployed candidates in the pipeline have dramatically higher conversion intent and should be prioritised for outreach.
City development index has a non-linear effect — both very high CDI (major tech hubs) and low CDI candidates switch at higher rates, but for different reasons. High-CDI candidates have more options; low-CDI candidates are motivated to relocate. Binning the CDI surfaced this U-shaped pattern that a continuous variable would flatten.
STEM candidates are significantly more mobile — major_discipline_STEM ranked highly across all model iterations. STEM-trained data scientists have broader market demand and switch jobs more readily than candidates from humanities or business backgrounds.
Recent job changers are likely to change again — last_new_job is a consistent top-10 feature. Candidates who changed jobs recently have demonstrated willingness to switch and face lower psychological switching costs. In recruiting, this is a reliable signal of active job-seeking intent.
Gender non-disclosure correlates with switching — gender_Unknown appeared consistently in the top 15. Rather than imputing this with the majority class, treating it as a meaningful category revealed a genuine behavioral pattern in the data.
Company type matters more than company size alone — both company_type_Unknown and company_type_Public Sector appeared in the top features. Public sector employees switch less; candidates with unknown employers (likely unemployed) switch most. The interaction between these variables was captured by the is_unemployed flag.
Treating missingness as signal outperformed imputation — for the three high-missingness columns (company_type, company_size, gender), coding missing values as meaningful categories rather than imputing produced the top features in the final model. This was only possible because missing data was not random — it reflected real candidate characteristics.
Feature engineering had the highest ROI of any intervention — three engineered features (is_unemployed, cdi_bin, exp_x_company_size) collectively account for ~38% of model importance and drove a 3.1 percentage point gain in Switch Recall, catching 35 additional job switchers per 3,832 candidates evaluated.

The final model achieves a ROC-AUC of 0.807 and Switch Recall of 77.1% on held-out test data. To further improve recall, the highest-value next steps would be: incorporating engagement signals (time-on-platform, course completion rates), adding resume-based features (total tenure, number of past roles), and exploring ensemble stacking with a logistic regression meta-learner.
